## Context

While internal quality measurement provided a view of how search was performing in absolute terms, the team needed to size and prioritize search pain points relative to a key competitor — and understand what users do after search fails. Without competitive context, it was difficult to create urgency around quality gaps or prioritize the product roadmap.

## Question

How do search failure rates and specific pain points compare to a leading competitor, and what do users do after a failed search — do they try again, leave, or seek alternatives?

## Approach

Partnered with a research foundations team to embed a dedicated search module in a quarterly off-platform user survey (N=5,000+ US adults):

- **Competitive benchmark design**: Designed a question flow capturing failure rates, specific pain points, and post-failure behavior for both the platform's search and a leading competitor's search
- **Pain point taxonomy**: Developed a detailed classification of search failure types — topic mismatch, result type mismatch, poor personalization, stale results, usability issues
- **Post-failure behavioral mapping**: Tracked what users did after experiencing a failed search: retry, abandon, switch to competitor, or use a different modality
- **Demographic analysis**: Analyzed failure patterns and post-failure behavior across age groups, usage frequency, and search type (content search vs. entity search)

## Key Findings

1. **Competitive gap concentrated in content search**: The largest quality gap versus the competitor was in content search (finding posts, articles, videos), not entity search (finding people, pages, groups) — creating clear prioritization for where to invest
2. **Prioritized pain point taxonomy**: Topic mismatch, result type mismatch, and poor personalization emerged as the top three pain points across both search types
3. **Age-based divergence in post-failure behavior**: Older users were significantly more likely to abandon search entirely after failure (a retention risk), while younger users sought alternative sources — informing differentiated recovery strategies
4. **Content searchers and younger adults disproportionately affected**: These segments suffered more from relevance, personalization, and usability issues — aligning with growth audience priorities

## Impact

- **Created urgency**: Competitive benchmarking data transformed internal conversations about search quality from "we're improving" to "we're behind in specific, quantified ways"
- **Prioritized the roadmap**: Pain point sizing and demographic analysis directly informed which problems to solve first and for which audiences
- **Informed retention strategy**: The finding that older users abandon after failure (rather than switching) highlighted a silent retention risk that engagement metrics wouldn't capture
- **Established a recurring benchmark**: The survey module became a quarterly tracking tool, enabling trend monitoring against the competitor

## What This Demonstrates

This project shows the ability to design research that creates strategic urgency — not just insight. Competitive benchmarking is powerful when it's specific (not "they're better" but "they're better at content search, specifically on relevance and personalization, and here's the user behavior impact"). It also demonstrates skill in partnering with other research teams to embed targeted questions in existing programs, maximizing insight per respondent.
