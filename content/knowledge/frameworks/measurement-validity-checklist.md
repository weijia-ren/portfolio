A practical checklist for evaluating whether a metric or measurement instrument actually measures what it claims to.

## Quick Validity Check

Before trusting any metric, run through these questions:

### 1. Face Validity
- [ ] Does the metric intuitively make sense for what we're trying to measure?
- [ ] Would a stakeholder understand what it captures?

### 2. Content Validity
- [ ] Does the metric cover all important aspects of the construct?
- [ ] Are we missing any dimensions?
- [ ] Is anything included that shouldn't be?

### 3. Construct Validity
- [ ] **Convergent**: Does it correlate with other measures of the same thing?
- [ ] **Discriminant**: Does it *not* correlate with measures of different things?
- [ ] **Known-groups**: Does it differentiate groups we know should differ?

### 4. Criterion Validity
- [ ] **Concurrent**: Does it agree with an established measure taken at the same time?
- [ ] **Predictive**: Does it predict future outcomes it should predict?

### 5. Reliability
- [ ] **Internal consistency**: Do sub-components agree? (Cronbach's alpha > 0.7)
- [ ] **Test-retest**: Is it stable over time when the underlying thing hasn't changed?
- [ ] **Inter-rater**: Do different observers/methods give similar results?

## Common Pitfalls

- **Goodhart's Law**: When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure
- **Proxy drift**: The proxy metric diverges from what you actually care about
- **Construct conflation**: Measuring two things at once and calling it one metric
- **Survivorship bias**: Only measuring users who stuck around

## When to use this

- Evaluating a new product metric
- Designing a survey instrument
- Reviewing someone else's measurement approach
- Building an AI evaluation framework
