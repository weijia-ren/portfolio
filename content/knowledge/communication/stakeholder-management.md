How to build research influence in a product organization.

## The Researcher's Influence Model

Research impact = Quality of insight × Strength of relationships × Timing

You can have the best insight in the world, but if the right people don't hear it at the right time, it doesn't matter.

## Stakeholder Mapping

For every research project, map your stakeholders:

| Role | What they need from you | How to engage |
|------|------------------------|---------------|
| **Decision-maker** (VP/Director) | Confidence that the decision is sound | Executive summary, 1-pager, bold recommendation |
| **Primary partner** (PM) | Actionable findings tied to their roadmap | Regular syncs, co-create research questions |
| **Builders** (Engineers, Designers) | Specific, concrete implications for their work | Design reviews, spec contributions, office hours |
| **Peers** (other researchers) | Methodological rigor, collaboration | Peer review, method shares, joint projects |

## Communication Cadence

| When | What | Format |
|------|------|--------|
| **Project start** | Align on research questions and decisions to inform | 30-min kickoff meeting |
| **Mid-project** | Share preliminary signals, adjust if needed | Informal check-in or Slack update |
| **Findings ready** | Full readout with recommendations | Presentation (30–45 min) |
| **Post-readout** | Track whether findings influenced decisions | Follow-up in 2–4 weeks |
| **Quarterly** | Research impact summary | Brief written summary to leadership |

## Building Trust Over Time

### The Trust Equation (Maister)
```
Trust = (Credibility + Reliability + Intimacy) / Self-Orientation
```

- **Credibility**: Know your domain deeply. Be the expert.
- **Reliability**: Deliver what you promise, when you promise it.
- **Intimacy**: Understand their pressures, goals, and constraints.
- **Low self-orientation**: Make it about their success, not your research.

### Practical Trust-Builders
- **Share bad news early** — don't surprise stakeholders with negative findings in a big meeting
- **Under-promise, over-deliver** — "I'll have preliminary findings by Friday" then deliver Thursday
- **Ask good questions** — "What decision would change if we found X vs. Y?" shows you understand their world
- **Close the loop** — follow up on how findings were used; celebrate when research drives decisions
- **Be honest about limitations** — acknowledging what you don't know builds more credibility than pretending certainty

## Handling Difficult Situations

### When your findings are unwelcome
1. Validate their perspective first: "I understand why the team expected X"
2. Present the evidence clearly, without judgment
3. Frame as "the data shows this; here are options for how to respond"
4. Never frame it as "you were wrong" — frame as "the data revealed something unexpected"

### When you're asked to validate a decision already made
1. Clarify: "Are we evaluating this decision, or have we already committed?"
2. If committed: reframe as "how do we optimize within this direction?"
3. If genuinely open: design research that could genuinely change the direction

### When research is deprioritized
1. Connect to what IS prioritized: "This research directly supports [their priority]"
2. Offer lightweight alternatives: "A quick pulse survey could answer this in 3 days"
3. Build a research backlog with visible prioritization criteria
4. Track and share the cost of NOT doing research (decisions made without evidence)

## The Research Operating System

Systematize your stakeholder practice:

- [ ] Maintain a research roadmap visible to partners
- [ ] Send a monthly/quarterly research impact summary
- [ ] Keep a "wins" log of decisions influenced by research
- [ ] Do quarterly stakeholder satisfaction check-ins
- [ ] Track research question → finding → decision → outcome pipeline
